We are interested in exploring propaganda techniques used in political messaging. We
focus specifically on the U.S. Members of the House’s policy statements as listed on 
their official websites. We webscraped the ‘Issues’ pages from these sites, which are 
all public and not under copyright. The resulting documents are text strings describing
the Representatives’ beliefs on various issues, including immigration, education, and
health care, among many others. We will do some further data processing to create 
uniform documents, each consisting of 3 adjacent sentences randomly sampled from the 
1,330 ‘Issues’ statements we scraped. 

For simplicity, we only aim to label the most commonly used propaganda techniques, which 
have been established by previous propaganda detection work. Informed by the results of 
Vorakitphan et al. in 2021, we will look for loaded language, name calling / labeling, 
appeal to fear / prejudice, and slogans in our documents. For a single document, we will 
annotate the binary presence or absence of each propaganda type, in addition to a 5th 
category, “no propaganda.” We will save these annotations for future reference, but for 
the purpose of this project we will discard any data samples with more than one positive 
label to avoid multilabel complications.

We will then convert our binary labels into class labels, where our discrete and 
exclusive classes are {loaded_language, name_calling_labeling, appeal_to_fear_prejudice,
slogans, no_propaganda}. Finally, we will train a classifier on this labeled data to 
detect these 4 types of propaganda.

There are existing propaganda detection pipelines for tweets from political candidates 
(Vorakitphan et al., 2021) and fear vs. anger mongering techniques from politicians 
(Friedrichs et al., 2022). However, these studies focus on social media platforms, an
inherently more polarizing space aimed at garnering engagement and interaction. On the 
other hand, the data source of our work (policy statements found on Representatives’ 
official websites) is meant to be informative. Thus, the analysis resulting from this 
body of work will demonstrate how the communication platform impacts politicians’ 
strategies for appealing to their constituents.
